Abel Selaocoe
from III. Scherzando vivace · Jun 5 · 6 suggestions
›
Your first encounter with Abel Selaocoe — a South African cellist, vocalist, and composer who fuses Bantu throat singing and African percussion with Baroque cello repertoire. This Bach Sarabande arrangement (by Fred Thomas for cello & string orchestra) comes from his sophomore album Hymns of Bantu (2025, Warner Classics), where it sits alongside traditional South African hymns and Giovanni Sollima's LBFiles suite. Selaocoe trained at the Royal Northern College of Music in Manchester and collaborates regularly with Manchester Collective, percussionist Bernhard Schimpelsberger, and djembe player Sidiki Dembélé — and Yo-Yo Ma guests on his debut album Where Is Home.
By Abel Selaocoe
💿 Hymns of Bantu (2025)
The full album this Sarabande comes from — 12 tracks weaving Bach and Marais with South African hymns, featuring Manchester Collective strings, Fred Thomas on prepared piano, and Dudù Kouaté on African percussion.
💿 Where Is Home / Hae ke Kae (2022)
Selaocoe's acclaimed debut on Warner Classics — features a collaboration with Yo-Yo Ma on 'Ibuyile I'Africa' and named one of the Best Albums of 2022 by Songlines magazine.
💿 Four Spirits (Live, 2025) — with Aurora Orchestra & Bernhard Schimpelsberger
Live recording with Aurora Orchestra and conductor Nicholas Collon — Selaocoe's improvising percussionist Bernhard Schimpelsberger joins for a fusion of orchestral and world music traditions.
Related Artists
🎛️ We Were Trees (2008) — Giovanni Sollima
The Sicilian cellist-composer who wrote the 'LBFiles' suite that Selaocoe performs — Sollima is a boundary-crossing cellist in his own right, blending classical with Mediterranean folk and improvisation.
🎛️ J.S. Bach: Cello Suites (2024) — Giovanni Sollima
Sollima's own reading of the complete Bach Cello Suites — a fascinating comparison with Selaocoe's reimagined Sarabande arrangement. Same repertoire, different cultural lens.
📻 Abel Selaocoe — The Complete Playlist (Warner Classics)
Official Warner Classics playlist spanning Selaocoe's full catalog — 35 tracks including both albums and singles, curated by the label.

Cleo Sol
from Rose in the Dark · Jun 4 · 9 suggestions
›
Cleo Sol's Rose in the Dark is where it all begins — her 2020 debut, entirely produced by her husband Inflo (Dean Josiah Cover), who also helms the mysterious UK collective SAULT and produced Michael Kiwanuka's Mercury Prize-winning KIWANUKA. Every Cleo Sol album lives on Inflo's Forever Living Originals label, making their partnership one of the most prolific artist-producer marriages in modern British soul. She's also featured on two Little Simz albums — "Selfish" on Grey Area (2019) and "Woman" on Sometimes I Might Be Introvert (2021), both also produced by Inflo — a triangle of London talent that keeps feeding itself.
By Cleo Sol
💿 Rose in the Dark (2020)
The album this track is from — her debut, all 11 tracks produced by Inflo. Play the whole thing.
💿 Mother (2021)
Her second album, also produced by Inflo, inspired by the birth of their child — intimate soul-jazz that deepens everything Rose in the Dark started.
💿 Heaven (2023)
Her surprise-released third solo album, continuing the Inflo-produced lineage into more devotional, spiritually-rooted territory.
💿 Gold (2023)
Released just two weeks after Heaven — a companion piece and her most sonically lush album to date.
Related Artists
🎛️ KIWANUKA (2019) — Michael Kiwanuka
Produced by Inflo, same as all Cleo Sol albums — won the Mercury Prize. If you love Inflo's warm, expansive production, this is essential.
🤝 Sometimes I Might Be Introvert (2021) — Little Simz
Cleo Sol features on "Woman" and Inflo produced the entire album — the same creative family behind Rose in the Dark.
🤝 UNTITLED (Black Is) (2020) — SAULT
Cleo Sol is the lead vocalist of SAULT, the secretive collective she runs with Inflo — this critically acclaimed album dropped the same year as Rose in the Dark.
🤝 5 (2019) — SAULT
SAULT's debut album with Cleo Sol on vocals — raw funk and soul that predates both Rose in the Dark and the collective's critical breakthrough.
📻 This is Cleo Sol
Community-curated playlist spanning her solo work and features — a good overview if you want the highlights.

Cécile McLorin Salvant
from ARTEMIS · Jun 4 · 7 suggestions
›
You heard her via ARTEMIS — the jazz supergroup with pianist Renee Rosnes, saxophonist Nicole Glover, trumpeter Ingrid Jensen, and bassist Noriko Ueda. But Salvant's solo career is one of the most decorated in modern jazz: three Grammy wins, a MacArthur "genius" grant, and a voice that channels Bessie Smith, Sarah Vaughan, and French chanson simultaneously. Born in Miami to a French mother and Haitian father, she studied Baroque music at the Darius Milhaud Conservatory in Aix-en-Provence before pivoting to jazz — that classical training shapes her theatrical phrasing and her obsession with obscure pre-war repertoire.
By Cécile McLorin Salvant
💿 Dreams and Daggers (2017)
Grammy-winning live album recorded at the Village Vanguard — captures her theatrical stage presence and risk-taking improvisation.
💿 Ghost Song (2022)
Her most ambitious album — original compositions alongside Kate Bush and Gregory Porter covers, with a haunted, cinematic production.
💿 Mélusine (2023)
Her French-language album exploring medieval myth — a radical departure that weaves Haitian Creole, Baroque ornamentation, and jazz.
Related Artists
🤝 ARTEMIS (2020) — ARTEMIS
The supergroup that brought you here — Salvant leads alongside Renee Rosnes, Nicole Glover, Ingrid Jensen, and Noriko Ueda. All-female jazz at the highest level.
🎛️ After Blue (2017) — Sarah Vaughan
The vocal ancestor Salvant most channels — Vaughan's late-career ballad work has the same dramatic range and harmonic daring.
🎛️ Alive at the Village Vanguard (2024) — Samara Joy
The next generation — Joy won the Grammy for Best New Artist and shares Salvant's love of the Great American Songbook, but with a warmer, more restrained instrument.
🤝 Written in the Stars (2018) — Renee Rosnes
ARTEMIS pianist's own album — elegant post-bop with Bill Charlap. The compositional mind behind the supergroup's arrangements.
